About

I am a doctoral candidate in Political Science at the University of Brasília, currently a visiting scholar in the Department of Political Science at the University of Illinois at Chicago. My dissertation examines how armed criminal governance reshapes electoral behavior in Brazilian cities, combining quantitative and qualitative methods. Beginning in 2026, I will join the Brazilian Center for Analysis and Planning (CEBRAP), in São Paulo, as a postdoctoral researcher.

My work centers on criminal governance, electoral behavior, political violence, and subnational political dynamics in Latin America, with particular attention to public security and racial inequality. I am a researcher at the Latin American Security and Defense Network (RESDAL) and affiliated with GEMAA (IESP-UERJ), and I serve on the editorial board of the Brazilian Journal of Political Science. I previously worked with the Igarapé Institute, and in 2024 I was selected for the British Academy Writing Academy. I hold Bachelor's and Master's degrees in Political Science from the University of Brasília, work in Portuguese, Spanish, and English, and was born in São Paulo.

Research

My dissertation, Voting under criminal rules, asks how criminal governance reshapes electoral participation and choice in Rio de Janeiro — bringing together spatial data, administrative records, and fieldwork to trace how armed control travels into the ballot box. Adjacent projects look at militia voting geographies, paramilitary politics, residents' associations under armed rule, and the racial geography of political violence.

Research & Consulting

I provide strategic political analysis for complex Latin American contexts, bridging academic research and practical decision-making. Areas of work include:

  • Political risk and institutional dynamics
  • Defense, public security, and organized crime
  • Human rights, gender and racial inequalities
  • Electoral landscapes and democratic trends
  • Policy evaluation and governance structures

I work with research centers and universities, NGOs and international cooperation agencies, and private-sector organizations across Latin America, integrating qualitative and quantitative methods — including spatial data analysis, institutional mapping, and strategic briefings.
